Calabrian chilis from southern Italy have a heat that's different from other hot peppers — it's fruity and rounded, building slowly rather than spiking immediately. Combined with sharp cheddar and the tang of sourdough, this loaf has become one of the most requested things I make.

It disappears fast. People who say they don't like spicy food eat two slices and ask what's in it.

## Formula

| Ingredient | Weight | Baker's % |
|---|---|---|
| Bread flour | 450g | 90% |
| Whole wheat flour | 50g | 10% |
| Water | 385g | 77% |
| Starter (active) | 100g | 20% |
| Fine sea salt | 9g | 1.8% |
| **Sharp cheddar, small dice** | **100g** | — |
| **Calabrian chili paste** | **2 tbsp (~30g)** | — |
| **Smoked paprika** | **1/2 tsp** | — |

Note: Salt is reduced slightly to 1.8% because aged cheddar already contributes saltiness.

## The Cheddar

Use a proper sharp cheddar — aged at least 12 months, ideally 18. Extra-sharp if you can find it. The intensity of flavor is important because the sourdough tang and chili both have strong personalities; a mild cheddar simply disappears.

Cut into small dice (roughly 1cm cubes) rather than grating. Grated cheddar melts completely into the crumb and loses its pockets; small cubes create defined molten areas when hot and glossy, yielding cheese pockets when cooled.

## The Calabrian Chili Paste

Calabrian chili paste (also labeled as *peperoncino calabrese* or *'nduja-adjacent paste*) is available at Italian grocery stores and increasingly at good supermarkets. It's a red, oil-based paste with a fruity heat and deep red color.

Two tablespoons gives a warm, persistent heat. For a milder version, reduce to one tablespoon. For genuine heat, increase to three — but this changes the character of the loaf significantly and it won't be for everyone.

Do not substitute with sriracha or generic hot sauce. The fruity, olive oil-based Calabrian paste behaves differently and contributes a very different flavor profile.

## Mixing

Mix your dough as normal. Before lamination, stir the Calabrian chili paste and smoked paprika together in a small bowl.

## Lamination

30–45 minutes into bulk fermentation:
1. Stretch the dough to a thin rectangle on a wet surface
2. Dot the chili paste mixture across the surface and use your fingers to spread it gently, leaving a 2cm border on each side
3. Scatter the cheddar cubes evenly
4. Fold and return to the bowl

The chili paste will begin to tint the dough a reddish-orange color as bulk fermentation progresses. This is expected and intentional.

## Bulk Fermentation

Monitor carefully. The chili paste can slightly inhibit yeast activity (capsaicin has mild antimicrobial properties at high concentrations). At 2 tablespoons per 500g of flour, the effect is minimal, but be prepared for the bulk to run a bit longer than usual — up to 6 hours instead of 5.

## Shaping and Baking

Shape as usual. The dough will be a warm orange-red color throughout.

Standard Dutch oven bake. The exterior will pick up more color than a plain loaf due to the chili paste on the surface — watch the last 10 minutes of uncovered baking to ensure it doesn't go past deeply bronzed.

## Serving

This loaf doesn't need much. A slice warm from a rested loaf is already complete. For occasions when you want to push it further:

- Alongside a bowl of roasted tomato soup
- As the base for a fried egg with hot honey
- With a smear of cream cheese — the cool, neutral dairy is a perfect counterpoint
- Toasted and rubbed with a cut garlic clove

It's also remarkable cold. The chili heat mellows slightly after a day, the cheddar firms back up, and the whole loaf becomes denser and more cohesive. Cold slice, thin smear of butter. That's it.
