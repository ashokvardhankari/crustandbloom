If there's one drink that taught me to take espresso seriously, it's the cappuccino. Not because it's the most complex — it isn't — but because every flaw in your espresso is laid bare by the simplicity of the milk. There's nowhere to hide.

The cappuccino has become my morning benchmark. I make one almost every day, and it tells me immediately whether I dialed in well or whether I should adjust the grind. It's honest in a way that masked drinks — sweet syrups, chocolate, heavy cream — can never quite be.

## What Makes a Cappuccino

Equal thirds: espresso, steamed milk, microfoam. That's the traditional definition, and while modern variations drift from it, I keep coming back to the classic ratio. Around 60ml of espresso, 60ml of milk, and a generous cap of aerated foam — all in a 180ml ceramic cup that's been pre-warmed.

The cup matters. A proper cappuccino cup is wider at the rim than a cortado glass, which allows the foam to rest as a thick, stable cap. I use a 180ml wide-mouth ceramic — it holds heat and gives the pour room to breathe.

## The Shot

18g in, 36g out, 25 seconds. The textbook 1:2 ratio, with extraction time somewhere between 24 and 27 seconds depending on the bean. A classic cappuccino benefits from a slightly shorter, more concentrated shot than a latte — the milk isn't as dominant, and you want the espresso to assert itself.

I grind fresh for every shot. With a cappuccino, the difference between grinding five minutes ahead and grinding fresh is audible in the cup — the aromatics fade quickly once ground, and a fresh shot has a brightness that defines the whole drink.

> The roast matters here more than in many milk drinks. I prefer a medium-light roast with good acidity — something with a stone fruit or dark chocolate note. Very dark roasts can turn bitter when paired with a small amount of milk.

## Steaming the Milk

Sixty degrees Celsius, not a degree more. This is the temperature I've landed on after making hundreds of cappuccinos. At 60°C, the milk sugars have begun to caramelize just enough to add sweetness, the foam is stable and glossy, and the drink is the right temperature to drink immediately without scalding your palate.

More air than a latte, less than a macchiato. I stretch the milk for about two seconds longer than I would for a flat white — inserting the steam tip just below the surface and pulling it slightly lower as the volume grows. The goal is a thick, pillowy microfoam that holds its shape when poured.

## The Pour

This is where practice compounds. The pour on a cappuccino is different from a latte — I tilt the cup more steeply and pour at a slightly higher height to break through the top layer of foam and allow the foam to float naturally above the milk. The result, when it works, is a clear defined cap of white foam sitting above the espresso-tinged milk.

I won't pretend my latte art is always perfect. Some mornings it is; some mornings it's a slightly tilted tulip and I drink it anyway, because the flavour doesn't care what the surface looks like.

## Notes

- **Whole milk** is my default for cappuccinos. The fat content gives a richer texture than oat milk and the foam holds longer.
- **Pre-warm everything** — cup, milk pitcher. Cold milk takes longer to steam and makes it harder to hit your target temperature.
- **Don't tap and swirl too aggressively**. You want to integrate the foam without knocking out all the air you just put in.
- Make it with intention. A cappuccino made slowly, with attention, always tastes better than one made in a hurry.
